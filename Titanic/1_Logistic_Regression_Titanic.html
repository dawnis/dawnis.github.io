Interesting! Survival was different depending on the embark location, with those boarding at Southampton being particularly unfotunate. It's likely that embark location is correlated with something else important such as passenger class or number of relatives aboard.

Since I planned on using a logistic regression model as a first pass, I decided to code embark location as dummy variables. This requires the formation of three more data features (one for each location), in which passengers are coded with a 1 if they embarked at that location and 0 otherwise.

Also, since it doesn't really make sense to code passenger class on a linear scale (is 3rd classs 3x 1st?), I also coded three dummy variables for passenger class in anticipation of includison into my model.

Looks good! At this point, we are ready to explore the data and make a preliminary model. I will ignore cabin number for now, since that information is missing for most of the passengers and it is more difficult to deal with (requiring the dreaded regular expression library).

In addition to the the features that I created dummy variables for, we will use Fare, SibSp (# of siblings/spouses aboard), and Parch (# of parents/children). Note that it is ambiguous who the related passengers are for the last two variables. These features are encoded into a predictor dataframe X, and survival into the dependent frame Y.

We should scale Age and Fare to the same range as the other predictors in our model to improve the performance of the logistic regression. To do this, we'll make a scaled and mean sutracted version of our predictor matrix for those columns.

We can see that survival per deck ranged from 10% of the records (Deck F) to 40% (Deck D). Looking at the distribution of room numbers for those that lived (black) vs. those that died (red) doesn't seem to suggest that a particular role for room # in survival. The scarcity of the data makes it difficult to judge whether the observed pattern is meaningful. We could compare the distribution of survival/death to a chi square distribution to determine statistical significance. However, since this information is present in roughly 25% of the data, and assuming the test set is similar in proportion the training set, we are probably better off ignoring it for the moment.

Our last order of business is to assess the difference in explained variance when some of the less significant parameters are removed. This will help protect against overfitting of the training set.
